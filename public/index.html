Mastering Drought using Statistical Models

Recents droughts in California have exposed the vulnerability of our water resources, especially the California reservoir system. New research from scientists at Caltech could help us predict water levels and make our water management more resilient to future droughts.

Drought in California

Drought in California: From 2012 to 2015, California was in a state of severe drought, on par with the worst 4-year periods in the past 1200 years. The impact of this drought included:

  • Sierra Nevada, California’s largest source of water runoff, was 5% of its normal amount.
  • 9 million fewer acre feet of surface water for irrigation, fallowing a half million acre of land and costing $1.85 billion for the department of agriculture
  • A State of Emergency with over $3 billion dollars allocated to drought relief

PDSI: Measuring Drought Severity

Palmer Drought Severity Index (PDSI) is a hydrological measure that uses temperature and precipitation data to estimate relative dryness, without using water level data. The scale of PDSI is from -5 to 5, where positive values indicate wet spells and negative values indicate dry spells. PDSI is used as a tool in water management, aiding the decision-making processes for drought mitigation and response. In 2015, we were at a PDSI value of -4, leading the state to take the following actions:

  • Governor declared state of emergency and created a task force
  • Initiated implementation of Initiate of Emergency Action Plan
  • Eliminate non-essential water usage, like outdoor water use

Currently, in 2018, we are at a PDSI value of 3.

Droughts Drain Reservoirs

California’s water comes from two main sources:

1. Groundwater: water in soil & rocks -- Accounts for 60% of our water

2. Surface water: water above ground, stored in 1530 reservoirs -- Accounts for 40% of our water

Reservoirs are man-made water storage that provide for agriculture, industry, power generation, and recreational needs. Reservoirs increase amount of standing available water by 700%. While larger cities have diverse water resources such as multiple rivers and reservoirs, some smaller cities are limited to a single, local reservoir for water.

Humans Control Reservoirs

Unlike most water systems which occur naturally (like lakes), humans play a significant role in the behavior of reservoirs. Every day, water managers decide how much water to release from reservoirs based on today’s demands and future needs. They do their best to manage water levels so that the reservoirs don’t dry out, but sometimes this happens anyway-- and there are consequences for communities when it does.

Running out in a Drought

In August 2014, the water level in Northern California's Lake Shastina Reservoir dropped critically low. This resulted in serious water restrictions in the nearby town of Montague, CA, affecting their 1300 residents.

  Because of the water shortage, each household in Montague was limited to 5,000 gallons of water a month. Families using more than this amount faced fines.

  The average household size in Montague in the 2010 census was 2.5. As it turns out, 5,000 gallons of water doesn't get the average family very far.

  If each family member takes a 10-minute shower every day, the average household will use 2,800 gallons of water in a month.

  If each person uses running water for 5 minutes every day, that's another 1,400 gallons per month.

  Flushing the toilet twice per day per person takes another 560 gallons of water.

  Just one load of dishes and one load of laundry per week will use 224 gallons of water.

  Even using water conservatively, the average household could easily use 5,000 gallons of water in a month. A family of three or more would almost surely face fines.

Predicting Reservoir Levels

The story of Montague demonstrates that communities face consequences when a reservoir reaches critically low levels. If we could predict the reservoir’s water level a couple months in advance, we could prevent this. But predicting future water levels is more difficult than it sounds.

Why can't water managers always tell when a reservoir is at risk? Let’s look at the Buchanan Reservoir, shown below.

In May 2013, the managers of Buchanan Reservoir had a critical decision to make. California was in the middle of a historical drought, the fill season was over, and they had to guess the likelihood of depletion just by looking at the reservoir’s history. Looking at the historical graph, their previous management techniques appeared to be sufficient to keep the reservoir running and satisfy local demands. So the managers chose to conduct “business as usual” and they let the water go.

Unfortunately, the drought worsened and the reservoir reached critical capacity. The reservoir was practically out of operation between 2014-2015.

Can we develop better techniques than this naive approach? To develop a good model of the reservoir response to drought conditions, we have to keep a few things in mind...

Reservoir levels depend on External Factors

Several external factors influence reservoir levels. For instance, reservoir levels correlate with future PDSI, temperature, precipitation and snowpack.

Reservoirs depend on other reservoirs.

It turns out that reservoirs are "connected"-- that is, there's a high correlation between the water levels of some pairs of reservoirs. Sometimes this is for obvious reasons (like they're physically connected), but sometimes there are statistical correlations (for example, from similar geography and water management). Taking into account these connections, as well as PDSI and other external factors, can help us predict how reservoirs will do.

Statistical models can help us predict what will happen to a reservoir.

Researchers at Caltech have developed a statistical model that incorporates reservoir dependency and the influence of external factors. The model is based on thinking of reservoirs as nodes in a graph and edges between nodes representing dependencies between reservoirs. The external factors are characterized as other nodes sitting in this graph impacting each reservoirs. Using historical data, Caltech researchers are able to precisely characterize dependencies of reservoirs and quantify the influence of external factor on reservoirs. One of these external factors is PDSI. Let’s see what this model tells us about the influence of PDSI on the reservoirs …

What we're measuring is risk.

This model measures the risk of reservoir exhaustion-- in other words, how likely it is that a particular reservoir will drop below x% capacity, given a PDSI of y. If a water manager were to use this model and see that the risk of their reservoir exhausting is high, they can make better informed decisions, and conserve water.

Conclusion

There's currently no standardized system for how a water manager should respond to a certain PDSI. This model could help water managers identify when a reservoir is at risk and take different actions to avoid situations like Montague's.
